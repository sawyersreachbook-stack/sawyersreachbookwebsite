The Full Teaser — Universal

The world is loud right now. AI is reshaping how we work. Automation is rewriting what a career even means. Politics feels like a war with no sides worth joining. Your phone knows more about your mood than your closest friend does. And somewhere in the middle of all of it, you're just trying to hold your family together, show up for your neighbors, and figure out what it means to be human in a world that seems increasingly designed for something else. Sawyer's Reach begins exactly there. The Carter family — Emily, Ben, and their three boys — live in a quiet corner of Wisconsin where the creek feeds the lake, the lake powers the town, and four generations of their family have called the same land home. Then something starts to change. Slowly. Quietly. The kind of change you don't notice until you do — and then can't unsee. A corporation called ZevraCorp has arrived with promises of progress, jobs, and a revolutionary wellness technology. The spring dries up. The town disappears from internet searches. Security drones patrol streets that used to belong to children. What follows is a thriller. But it's also something rarer: a love story about a family that refuses to be optimized out of existence. Ben and Emily are not heroes in any traditional sense. They're parents doing their best in circumstances they didn't choose, trying to keep the anxiety out of their voices for their kids' sake. The neighbors who rally around them aren't soldiers or saviors — they're a diner owner, a store clerk, a camp counselor, and a retired lineman who still knows how the power grid works. Ordinary people who still believe that this is our home, and we're here to protect it. Sawyer's Reach was written for the moment we're in — not to frighten you, but to remind you of something the noise keeps drowning out: the future isn't decided by the most powerful corporation or the smartest AI. It's decided by people who choose each other. By families who face hard things together instead of alone. By communities that remember what they're actually for. The book ends the way the best things end — not with a perfect world, but with a better one. With bikes on freshly paved roads. With bakery doors wide open and every bench full. With a campfire, and stories, and voices rising together in familiar songs. It earns that ending. Every page of it. If you've been hungry for a story that sees today's world clearly and still chooses hope — this is the one.

Website Newsletter
From the Author — Nick's Voice

I've spent more than two decades in classrooms. Lately, the anxiety I see in my students is different — heavier, more specific. They're not afraid of abstract futures. They're afraid of this one, the one being assembled around them right now, by forces they can see but feel powerless to affect. I wrote Sawyer's Reach because I needed to believe something — and I needed them to believe it too. That the story doesn't end with the most powerful corporation or the most sophisticated AI winning. That the things we're most afraid of losing are also, and have always been, the things capable of saving us. The Carters are a fictional family. But the love they carry — the stubbornness with which they refuse to be separated from each other and from the community that shaped them — that part is true. I borrowed it from real life. From my wife, who is the bravest person I know. From my boys, who remind me every day what actually matters. From the teachers, neighbors, and ordinary people I've watched quietly hold things together when everything else was falling apart. This book is for all of them. And for anyone who needs a reminder that the answer to an overwhelming world has never been a better algorithm. It's always been the person beside you who refuses to let go. — Nick Duda, author of Sawyer's Reach

Author Bio Page Newsletter
The Thriller Hook — With the Humanity Reveal

On the surface, Sawyer's Reach is a thriller. A corporation moves into a quiet Wisconsin town with promises of progress and a revolutionary AI wellness product. A family starts noticing things that don't add up. Drones appear in the sky. Security forces patrol streets that used to belong to children. And then things get much, much worse. But that's not what the book is actually about. What it's about is what a family does when the pressure becomes almost unbearable. How they talk to their kids about fear without passing it on. How neighbors who barely know each other find reasons to stand together in the dark with nothing but a mesh radio and the conviction that this place is worth protecting. How an ordinary dad — not a hero, not a genius, just a man who loves his people — becomes exactly what his family and his community need him to be. In an age of AI anxiety, political exhaustion, and the creeping sense that the world is being decided without us, Sawyer's Reach asks a simple question: what if the answer was never a better algorithm? What if it was just us — messy, stubborn, irreplaceable us — refusing to let go of each other? The answer, it turns out, is more powerful than anything ZevraCorp ever built.
